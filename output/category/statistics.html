Wash Your Hands Better… or Don’t!

Published: Thu 14 April 2016
By BobTodd

In Statistics.

A caring parent sent me the other day a link in The Telegraph to an article entitled “Why you've probably been washing your hands all wrong – study“. The article compares the typical method of running one’s palms over the back of one’s hands to a more robust 6-step method that includes interleaving fingers and a few other extra motions designed, presumably, to distribute the soap more completely. But in the midst of the article I noticed this passage:

They found the six-step technique was determined to be microbiologically more effective for reducing the median bacterial count (3.28 to 2.58) compared to the three-step method (3.08 to 2.88). However, using the six-step method required 25 per cent more time to complete.

So let’s think about this a minute. Let’s compare these two “median bacterial count” ranges. Just to get an idea of the change we’re talking about, let’s reduce each range to a single number. For simplicity, let’s work with the most charitable figures, the lower limits: 2.58 and 2.88. There’s a noticeable change there, but how big is it? Well, as a proportion of the bacterial count we presumably start with (assuming we wash our hands the “old” way), we have a change of (2.58 - 2.88)/2.88 = -0.3/2.88 = -0.1, or roughly a 10% reduction.

According to the article, then, we should spend 25% more time washing our hands for only a 10% reduction of the bacterial count. Color me unimpressed.

But now go back and look a little closer. Note that the median bacterial count range for the 3-step method (between 3.08 and 2.88) is completely contained within the median bacterial count range for the 6-step method (between 3.28 — a larger upper limit — and 2.58). So it looks like the 6-step method is indeed able to make your hands cleaner… or dirtier!
